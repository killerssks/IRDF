The Academic Threat Landscape

Before we touch any tools, we need to understand the why. Security measures that feel like bureaucratic inconveniences start making intuitive sense once you appreciate the threat environment you are operating in every single day.

Why Universities Are Prime Targets

Universities represent a uniquely attractive proposition for attackers. Think about what sits on a typical university network: cutting-edge research that may be worth hundreds of millions of rupees in intellectual property, personally identifiable information (PII) for tens of thousands of students and staff, health records from campus medical facilities, financial data, payment systems, and government-funded grant data. For an attacker, a university is a department store of sensitive information — all under one digital roof.

But the value of the data alone does not explain why universities are attacked more frequently than, say, a comparable private enterprise. The structural factors are just as important:

Factor The Academic Reality Why Attackers Love It
Open culture Academic freedom values open sharing, collaboration, and accessible networks More entry points, less friction for lateral movement inside networks
Diverse user base Students, faculty, admin staff, visiting researchers, contractors — all with different security awareness levels The weakest link is easier to find and exploit
Legacy infrastructure Universities operate century-old systems alongside bleeding-edge research tools Unpatched, legacy systems are a hacker's favourite playground
High-value IP Research in pharma, defence, AI, materials science — often government-funded Nation-state actors specifically target research for espionage
Budget constraints IT security teams are often under-resourced compared to private sector peers Fewer defences per surface area of network to protect
BYOD culture Students and faculty use personal devices on university networks Unmanaged devices introduce unknown vulnerabilities constantly
⚠️ Real-World Context In 2021, a cyberattack on the University of the Highlands and Islands (UHI) in Scotland forced the closure of all 13 campuses and their physical libraries simultaneously. In 2023, several Indian universities reported data breaches exposing student examination records and personal information. These are not isolated incidents — academic institutions globally reported a 75% increase in cyberattacks between 2020 and 2023.

The threat is not only external. Research shows that a significant proportion of security incidents in academic institutions result from insider actions — not malicious insiders, but well-meaning faculty, students, and staff who click a phishing link, use a weak password, or inadvertently misconfigure a cloud storage bucket. This is why security awareness is not just an IT problem: it is everyone's problem, and therefore everyone's opportunity to help.

The CIA Triad — The Foundation of Everything

Every framework, standard, and regulation in the entire field of information security traces its roots back to three core principles, collectively called the CIA Triad. Once you understand these three ideas, you have a mental model that unlocks almost all security conversations.

🔒

Confidentiality

Information should only be accessible to those who are authorised to see it. Keep secrets secret.

✅

Integrity

Information should be accurate and unaltered. Data should only be changed by authorised people through authorised processes.

🌐

Availability

Systems and information should be accessible when authorised users need them. Downtime is a security failure.

The Grading System Analogy

Abstract concepts land differently when they connect to something familiar. Consider your university's examination and grading system — you interact with it every semester:

CIA Principle Grading System Example Breach Scenario
🔒 Confidentiality Only the student, their faculty, and the exam controller should see a student's grade. A student's grade list leaks on a WhatsApp group before official results. Private information is now public.
✅ Integrity A grade of 78 should remain 78 — not altered by anyone after entry into the system. A malicious actor hacks the Student Information System and changes a grade from 45 to 75. The data is now untrustworthy.
🌐 Availability The exam portal must be online and functional when students need to check results or register for courses. A Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack crashes the exam portal during the result declaration period. Authorised users cannot access what they are entitled to.
📌 Teaching Moment — The Triangle of Trade-Offs Security professionals often have to consciously balance these three principles against each other. Maximising Confidentiality (encrypting everything) can sometimes reduce Availability (slowing systems down). Maximising Availability (making systems as accessible as possible) can sometimes weaken Confidentiality. Real-world security is always a negotiation between these three demands.

Multi-Factor Authentication & Zero Trust

Understanding the CIA Triad tells us what we are protecting. MFA and Zero Trust tell us how we protect it.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Authentication is the process of proving you are who you say you are. Traditionally, a single password was considered sufficient. The problem: passwords can be stolen, guessed, or leaked in data breaches — often without the owner knowing. MFA solves this by requiring proof from two or more distinct categories of evidence:

Factor Category What it Means Common Examples
🧠 Something you Know A secret only you should know Password, PIN, security question answer
📱 Something you Have A physical object or device in your possession OTP via SMS, Google Authenticator app, hardware security key (YubiKey)
👁️ Something you Are A biometric characteristic unique to you Fingerprint scan, facial recognition, iris scan

Even if an attacker steals your password (the "something you know"), they still cannot log in without the second factor — typically your phone. This single control neutralises the most common attack vectors, including phishing, credential stuffing, and brute force attacks.

💡 Analogy: The ATM You have been using MFA for decades without thinking about it. An ATM requires your debit card (something you have) AND your PIN (something you know). Even if someone knows your PIN, they cannot withdraw money without the physical card — and vice versa. Email accounts and university portals deserve the same level of protection as your bank account.

Zero Trust Architecture — "Never Trust, Always Verify"

Traditional network security assumed that everything inside the network perimeter was trustworthy. Once you were on the university Wi-Fi, the assumption was that you were legitimate. Zero Trust discards this assumption entirely. Its core principle: no user, device, or system should be trusted by default — regardless of where they are or how they got in.

Every request for access is verified every time. Access is granted only to the minimum resources needed for a specific task (the principle of "least privilege"). Behaviour is continuously monitored for anomalies.

In an academic context: even if a faculty member is connected to the campus LAN and has a valid username and password, a Zero Trust system might also check whether their device is up-to-date, whether the login time and location are normal, and whether the resources they're trying to access are consistent with their usual patterns.

The Human Firewall — Your Most Important Security Control

A firewall is a security system that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on rules. Technical firewalls are essential — but they cannot stop an attack that a human unwittingly invites in.

When a faculty member clicks a phishing link, downloads a malicious attachment, or shares their password over email, the most sophisticated technical defences become irrelevant. Verizon's Data Breach Investigations Report consistently finds that 74–82% of all data breaches involve a human element. Humans are simultaneously the greatest vulnerability and the most potent defence in any security system.

The concept of the "Human Firewall" refers to a workforce that is security-aware, sceptical of unsolicited communications, and empowered to recognise and report threats. You — as a faculty member who has just attended this FDP — are becoming part of your institution's human firewall right now.

⚠️ Social Engineering is the Preferred Attack Vector Attackers find it far easier and cheaper to manipulate a human than to break through a technical control. Phishing emails, pretexting phone calls, and baiting USB drives are all examples of social engineering — exploiting human psychology (trust, urgency, fear, helpfulness) rather than technical weaknesses. Understanding this is your first and most powerful line of defence.

Lab 1: The Credential Check

Tool: haveibeenpwned.com  ·  Estimated Time: 10 minutes

✦ Live Demo

What is a Data Breach?

A data breach occurs when an attacker gains unauthorised access to a system and extracts sensitive data — typically usernames and passwords. These stolen credential databases are then traded, sold, or published on the dark web.

Here is the critical, often-overlooked detail: the breach of Platform A means your other accounts are also at risk. Why? Because most people reuse passwords. If your email and password for an online shopping site are stolen, and you use the same email and password for your university account, an attacker can log straight into your university systems — even though the university was never breached. This attack is called credential stuffing, and it is one of the most common account takeover methods used today.

About haveibeenpwned.com (HIBP)

HIBP was created by Troy Hunt, a globally recognised security researcher. It is now a trusted public resource, officially partnered with the FBI and UK National Crime Agency. The site holds a database of over 13 billion compromised accounts from thousands of data breaches. It allows you to search for your email address and see whether it appears in any known breach — without exposing your data further.

💡 Privacy Note Entering your email address into HIBP does not expose it in any new way. The service uses a technique called "k-anonymity" to check passwords without sending your actual password to their server. Your email address is searched against their database and results are shown to you — that's it. Troy Hunt's methodology and the site's privacy policy are publicly documented.
📸 [Insert Screenshot: The haveibeenpwned.com homepage showing the large search bar in the centre with the text "Email address or phone number" and the "pwned?" button. The site's clean, dark interface should be visible.]

Step-by-Step: Checking Your Credentials

1

Navigate to haveibeenpwned.com in your browser. You should see a large search bar centred on the page. This is the entire interface — no login required, no account creation.

2

Type your primary email address into the search bar. This should be the email address you use most often for online registrations — likely the one associated with your university account, a Google account, or LinkedIn.

3

Click the "pwned?" button (or press Enter). The page will display one of two results — we'll interpret both below.

4

Read through the breach listing carefully. For each breach listed, note: the name of the breached service, the date it occurred, and — critically — what data was compromised (passwords, phone numbers, physical addresses, etc.).

5

Optional but recommended: Search any other email addresses you use regularly — personal Gmail, old college email, business email. Each one may have a different breach history.

Interpreting Your Results

✅ Green Screen — "Good news — no pwnage found!" Your email address was not found in any breach in the HIBP database. This is excellent news! However, it does not mean your data has never been breached — only that it hasn't appeared in the breaches known to HIBP. Maintain good password hygiene regardless.
🚨 Red Screen — "Oh no — pwned!" Your email address appears in one or more known data breaches. Do not panic. This is more common than most people expect — over 50% of participants in this type of exercise discover their email in at least one breach. What matters now is what you do next.

If You've Been Pwned — Immediate Action Plan

1

Identify which services were breached. Read each breach listing. HIBP tells you exactly which platform was compromised and when. Focus first on breaches that exposed passwords — look for "Passwords" in the "Compromised data" field.

2

Change your password on each breached service immediately. Log in to each service listed and change your password. If you can no longer access the account, use the "Forgot Password" flow. If the service no longer exists, skip it — just note which password it used.

3

Audit password reuse. Ask yourself: did I use the same password from that breached service anywhere else? If yes — your university portal, your email, your banking — change those passwords too, right now. This is the most urgent step.

4

Enable MFA on your most critical accounts. After changing passwords, enable MFA on your email account and your university account at minimum. These are the keys to your digital kingdom — treat them accordingly.

5

Consider a password manager. The root cause of credential stuffing attacks is password reuse, which itself is caused by the impossibility of remembering dozens of unique, complex passwords. A password manager (Bitwarden is free and open-source; 1Password is excellent for institutions) solves this problem completely. Ask your IT department about institutional licensing.

Lab 2: The Cyber Swiss Army Knife

Tool: gchq.github.io/CyberChef  ·  Estimated Time: 25 minutes

✦ Interactive

What is CyberChef?

CyberChef is a web application created and open-sourced by GCHQ (the UK's Government Communications Headquarters — their equivalent of the NSA). It describes itself as "a simple, intuitive web app for carrying out all manner of 'cyber' operations within a web browser." In practice, it is an extraordinarily powerful tool for transforming, encoding, encrypting, decoding, and analysing data — all in real time, in your browser, without any data leaving your machine.

Before we use it, we need to understand the difference between three terms that are used interchangeably in everyday conversation but mean very different things in security.

Encoding vs. Encryption vs. Hashing — The Critical Distinction

Encoding

Transforms data into a different format for compatibility purposes — not security. Anyone who knows the encoding scheme can instantly reverse it.

Like: Translating English to Morse code. Anyone with the codebook can decode it instantly.

Reversible — No Key

Encryption

Transforms data into an unreadable form to protect confidentiality. Only someone with the correct key can reverse it. Without the key, the data is computationally infeasible to read.

Like: A locked safe. You need the right key (or combination) to open it.

Reversible — Key Required

Hashing

Creates a unique, fixed-length "fingerprint" of data. It is a one-way function — you cannot reverse a hash to get the original data. Used to verify integrity, not protect secrecy.

Like: A fingerprint. You can verify it matches, but you cannot reconstruct the person from the fingerprint.

One-Way — No Reversal
⚠️ The Common Misconception That Gets People Hacked Many people — and some poorly-designed systems — confuse encoding with encryption. Base64 encoding (used to attach files in emails, embed images in web pages) is not encryption. If someone sends you "confidential" data encoded in Base64 thinking it is protected, it is not. Any person with access to a browser can decode it in seconds. Security depends on encryption with a secret key, not on obscuring data in a different format.
📸 [Insert Screenshot: The CyberChef interface showing its three-panel layout — the "Input" box on the right, the "Output" box below it, and the "Operations" panel on the left where recipes can be searched and dragged. Annotate each panel clearly.]

Navigating CyberChef

CyberChef uses a "recipe" metaphor. You put your Input data in the input box (top right), add one or more Operations from the left panel (search or browse by category), and the Output appears automatically in the output box (bottom right). You can chain multiple operations together, like steps in a recipe.

Exercise 2A — Encoding: Base64

Scenario: You receive a suspicious email claiming to be from your IT department. Embedded in the email is a block of text that starts with SGVsbG8gV29ybGQ=. Is this encrypted? Let's find out.

1

Go to your CyberChef tab: gchq.github.io/CyberChef

2

In the Input box (top right), type or paste this text exactly:
SGVsbG8gV29ybGQ=

3

In the Operations search bar (top left), type From Base64. You will see an operation called "From Base64" appear in the results.

4

Drag the "From Base64" operation into the Recipe area (the middle column). The output will update automatically.

5

Observe the Output. You should see: Hello World. That is all that was hidden — a completely trivial message. This demonstrates that Base64 provides zero security. It only encodes for compatibility, not confidentiality.

💡 Now try encoding something yourself Clear the recipe and Input. Search for To Base64 and drag it in. Type your name in the Input box. Notice how the output changes character by character as you type — this is encoding in real-time. There is no key, no secret. Anyone can reverse it instantly.

Exercise 2B — Encryption: AES

Scenario: You need to share sensitive student grade data with a colleague via email. You want to ensure that even if the email is intercepted, the data cannot be read. This requires real encryption.

1

Clear the CyberChef recipe and Input. In the Operations search bar, type AES Encrypt and drag the "AES Encrypt" operation into the Recipe area.

2

In the AES Encrypt operation settings that appear, you will see fields for Key and IV (Initialisation Vector). For this exercise, set the Key to:
MySecretKey12345
Change the Key format to "UTF8". Set Mode to "CBC".

3

In the Input box, type a message that represents sensitive data:
Student: Priya Sharma, Roll No: 2023CS042, Final Grade: 87

4

Observe the Output. You will see a string of seemingly random characters — this is the ciphertext. It is computationally infeasible to read without the key MySecretKey12345.

5

Now try to decrypt it: Replace the "AES Encrypt" operation with "AES Decrypt" (search for it). Use the same key. Paste the ciphertext as input. You should get your original message back. Change even one character of the key — and the decryption fails completely.

📸 [Insert Screenshot: CyberChef showing the AES Encrypt operation with the Key field filled in, the Input containing the student data string, and the Output showing a jumbled ciphertext string. Annotate with arrows showing "Original Data → Encryption with Key → Unreadable Ciphertext".]

Exercise 2C — Hashing: MD5 and SHA-256

Scenario: You download a research dataset from a journal repository. The website lists its MD5 and SHA-256 "checksums." What are these, and why do they matter?

1

Clear the recipe. Search for MD5 and drag the "MD5" operation into the Recipe. In the Input box, type:
The exam is on Tuesday at 10am

2

Note the MD5 hash in the Output. It will be a 32-character hexadecimal string — something like 7a8f3c.... This is the "fingerprint" of your exact input.

3

The Avalanche Effect: Now change a single character in the Input — change "Tuesday" to "tuesday" (lowercase t). Observe the Output. The hash changes completely. This is the avalanche effect: even a tiny change in input produces a completely different hash output.

4

Now try SHA-256: Replace the MD5 operation with a "SHA2-256" operation. Use the same input. Notice the hash is now 64 characters long — a stronger fingerprint. This is what most modern systems use for password storage and file verification.

5

Why does this matter? When you download a file, its legitimate distributor publishes its SHA-256 hash. After downloading, you hash the file and compare. If the hashes match exactly, the file was not tampered with in transit. If they differ by even one bit, the file has been modified — potentially by a malicious actor inserting malware.

📌 How Websites Store Your Password — A Peek Behind the Curtain When you create an account on a website, a well-designed system does not store your password. Instead, it hashes it (typically with SHA-256 or bcrypt) and stores the hash. When you log in later, it hashes your input and compares hashes. Even if the database is stolen, attackers see only hash strings — not your actual password. This is also why websites say "We cannot recover your password" — they don't have it. Only the hash exists.
⚠️ MD5 is Broken — Use SHA-256 or Better MD5 is still widely used for non-security purposes (checksums, data deduplication), but it is cryptographically broken and should never be used to store passwords or for any security-critical hashing. SHA-256 (part of the SHA-2 family) is the current standard. If you ever see a system using MD5 for password storage, flag it to your IT security team immediately.

Lab 3: X-Raying Phishing Emails

Tool: Google Admin Toolbox Message Header Analyser  ·  Estimated Time: 20 minutes

✦ Real Email

Understanding the Invisible Layer — Email Headers

Every email you have ever sent or received carries an invisible layer of metadata called headers. These headers document the complete journey of the email from sender to recipient — every mail server it passed through, timestamps at each hop, authentication results, and the technical identity of the original sender.

The email client you use (Gmail, Outlook, your university webmail) deliberately hides this information to keep the interface clean. But attackers rely on this invisibility when constructing phishing emails. By revealing and reading the headers, you can expose the true origin of any email — even if the "From" field has been spoofed to display a trusted name.

The Anatomy of an Email Header

Headers are read bottom-up — the bottom-most Received: line is where the email entered the network (closest to the original sender), and the top-most is the last server it passed through before reaching you. Attackers cannot forge the top Received headers, because those are added by servers you trust — your own mail provider. But they can forge everything else, including the From: field.

Header FieldWhat It Tells YouCan It Be Faked?
From:The sender's display name and address✅ Yes — easily spoofed
Reply-To:Where replies will go (often different from From in phishing)✅ Yes
Received:Each mail server the email passed through (chain of custody)⚠️ Lower entries can be faked; top entry cannot
Return-Path:Where delivery failure notices go — often reveals true sender domain⚠️ Often inconsistent in phishing
SPF / DKIM / DMARCAuthentication results — did the email actually come from the claimed domain?❌ No — these are verified by your mail server
Message-ID:Unique identifier for the email — the domain after the @ should match the From domain⚠️ Often inconsistent in phishing

Spotting a Spoofed Email — What to Look For

🚨 Red Flag 1: Mismatched Domains The From: header says registrar@university.ac.in, but the Return-Path: or the lowest Received: header shows a completely different domain — e.g., mail.suspiciousdomain.xyz. The sender is pretending to be your university but the email actually originated elsewhere.
🚨 Red Flag 2: SPF / DKIM Failures In the authentication results section of the headers (or as shown by the Toolbox), look for lines like spf=fail or dkim=fail. These mean the email failed the digital authentication checks your mail server performed. A legitimate email from university.ac.in will show spf=pass dkim=pass dmarc=pass.
⚠️ Red Flag 3: Unusual Routing Paths A legitimate internal university email should have a simple, short Received chain — maybe two or three hops between the sender's mail server and yours. A phishing email is sometimes routed through multiple obscure servers in different countries to obscure its origin. More than four to five hops for a routine email warrants scrutiny.
💡 Alternative Tool: MXToolbox Email Header Analyser If the Google Admin Toolbox is unavailable, navigate to mxtoolbox.com/EmailHeaders.aspx and paste your headers there. It provides a similar visual breakdown with colour-coded alerts for suspicious findings.

Quick Reference — Phishing Email Checklist

  • Hover over all links before clicking. Check the URL in the status bar — does it match the legitimate domain?
  • Check the sender's actual email address, not just the display name. The name can say "University IT Helpdesk" while the address is helpdesk@random-domain.com.
  • Look for urgency language: "Your account will be deleted in 24 hours," "Immediate action required." Legitimate institutions rarely communicate this way.
  • Watch for generic greetings: "Dear User," "Dear Faculty Member" instead of your actual name.
  • Examine email header Return-Path domain. Does it match the From domain?
  • Check SPF, DKIM, and DMARC results in headers. Any failure is a serious warning sign.
  • When in doubt — do not click. Forward to security@yourinstitution.ac.in or your IT helpdesk for verification.

Bringing It to the Classroom

The most effective security awareness happens when concepts are not siloed in a one-time IT presentation, but woven into the fabric of what students already care about. You — as a faculty member — are uniquely positioned to contextualise information security within your discipline in ways no IT department can replicate.

📌 Why This Matters for Your Students Your students will graduate into a world where data literacy and security awareness are professional baseline expectations — regardless of their field. A business student who understands data breach liability, a law student who knows digital evidence chain of custody, a healthcare student aware of patient data confidentiality — these students are more employable, more responsible, and more resilient professionals.

Cross-Discipline Integration Ideas

You do not need to redesign your course. A single well-placed case study, a 15-minute lab exercise, or a reflective assignment can plant the seed. Here are specific, immediately applicable ideas by discipline:

Business & Management

TopicSecurity IntegrationSuggested Activity
Corporate Governance Data breach liability, GDPR/DPDP Act compliance, board-level security responsibility Case study analysis: Sony Pictures hack (2014) or the Air India breach (2021) — board decisions and financial consequences.
Marketing & Consumer Behaviour How companies collect and monetise user data; dark patterns; informed consent Students audit a company's privacy policy and present their findings: What data is collected? Who is it shared with? Is the consent genuine?
Supply Chain Management Third-party vendor risk; supply chain attacks (e.g., SolarWinds) Map a supply chain and identify at which vendor a data leak would propagate furthest. Discuss mitigation strategies.
Entrepreneurship Security-by-design for startups; cost of a breach vs. cost of prevention Students add a "Security & Privacy" section to their business plan — what data do they collect, how do they protect it, what's their breach response plan?

Law & Legal Studies

TopicSecurity IntegrationSuggested Activity
Evidence & Procedure Digital evidence admissibility; chain of custody for electronic records; hashing for document integrity (the SHA-256 you just learned!) Students discuss: How does a hash value prove a digital document has not been altered since evidence collection? What happens if the hash changes?
Privacy Law India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023, GDPR comparison, data principal rights Mock scenario: A university suffers a student data breach. Students draft the breach notification letter that must be sent to affected individuals under the DPDP Act.
Cyberlaw / IT Act Sections 43, 66, 66C of the IT Act 2000; cybercrime classifications; jurisdiction Case study moot court: Prosecute or defend an accused in a credential stuffing case.

Healthcare & Life Sciences

TopicSecurity IntegrationSuggested Activity
Medical Ethics Patient data confidentiality (the "C" in CIA Triad); consent for data sharing in research; de-identification of health data Discuss: A hospital's Electronic Health Record system is hit by ransomware (Availability attack). Doctors cannot access patient records. What ethical decisions must be made in the next 60 minutes?
Public Health / Epidemiology Data integrity in surveillance systems; consequences of manipulated outbreak data Thought experiment: If a bad actor corrupts the Integrity of a disease surveillance database, altering case counts, what downstream decisions are affected? Who is harmed?
Clinical Research IRB data security requirements; encrypted data sharing between research sites; audit trails Students review an IRB consent form and identify all security and privacy commitments made to participants. Are they realistic? Are they enforceable?

Humanities, Social Sciences & Education

TopicSecurity IntegrationSuggested Activity
Media Studies / Journalism Source protection; operational security for investigative reporters; verifying digital evidence Students learn to verify an image's metadata (EXIF data) to check for manipulation — a real skill for digital journalists.
Sociology / Political Science Surveillance capitalism; digital authoritarianism; data and power asymmetries Essay: How does differential access to encryption tools create power imbalances between individuals, corporations, and states?
Education / Pedagogy Student data privacy in EdTech tools; FERPA/institutional data policies; safe digital learning environments Before adopting any new EdTech tool, students apply a simple checklist: What data does it collect? Where is it stored? Who can access it? Can it be deleted?

Look at your existing syllabus — where does a case study, a data scenario, or an ethical question fit naturally?

Your 3-Step Personal Security Action Plan

Theory and classroom ideas matter — but so does your own security posture. Here are three concrete actions you can take in the next 48 hours. Each takes less than 30 minutes.

01

Secure Your Accounts

Enable MFA on your university email, personal email, and any accounts that handle student or research data. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Authy) rather than SMS where possible. Change any password that appeared in the HIBP check today — use unique passwords for every account.

02

Adopt a Password Manager

The most friction-free way to have unique, strong passwords everywhere is a password manager. Start with Bitwarden — it is free, open-source, and has been independently audited. Import your existing passwords, then let it generate new unique ones as you change compromised passwords. Your institution may offer 1Password or LastPass — check with IT.

03

Teach One Colleague

The human firewall grows one person at a time. Share this webbook link with one colleague who was not at today's FDP. Walk them through the HIBP check together — five minutes of your time could protect critical institutional data. Consider advocating with your department head for this FDP to be an annual event.

📌 Institutional Reporting: Close the Loop If you receive a suspicious email, identify a potential phishing attempt, or discover a possible security incident: report it to your IT Security team immediately. Do not delete it — forward it with headers intact. Your report could protect hundreds of colleagues who received the same email but haven't noticed yet. You are now part of the human firewall — that responsibility is as important as the technical skills.
